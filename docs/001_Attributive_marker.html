Attributive marker

Asya Alekseeva

Last update: 2024-01-20

Attributive marker can be either -dɨ, either -d. We asked nine contexts, six with the head ‘knife’: ‘(my) brother’s knife’ (dependent noun in attributive, head in nominative), ‘I slaughtered a sheep with (my) brother’s knife’ (dependent noun in attributive, head in oblique case), ‘sharp knife’ (dependent adjective, head in nominative), ‘He slaughtered a sheep with a sharp knife’ (dependent adjective, head in oblique case), ‘my knife’ (dependent personal pronoun in attributive, head in nominative), ‘He slaughtered a sheep with my knife’ (dependent personal pronoun in attributive, head in oblique case). And three contexts with the head ‘dog’: ‘lame dog’ (dependent adjective), ‘my dog’ (dependent personal pronoun in attributive), ‘(my) brother’s dog’ (dependent noun in attributive). The difference between the words ‘knife’ and ‘dog’ is that ‘knife’ is a noun of 4th gender class, and ‘dog’ — of 3rd gender class.
